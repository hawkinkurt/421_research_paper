\subsection{Data Limitations}
\label{sec:lim_data}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

Several IMDb measurement issues affect how bilateral pairs are constructed. For films listing multiple countries of origin, the proportion of production attributable to each country is not available, while for filming destinations only the primary country is listed, so destinations that have specialised in serving as secondary locations will likely systematically undercounted. Film budgets are missing for approximately 53\% of films in the sample, so the budget regressions are likely biased toward larger studio productions for which budget figures are publicly reported. The selection into the budget subsample is plausibly correlated with the production characteristics that also determine incentive responsiveness, so the budget-margin estimates should be interpreted as conditional on this potential selection mechanism.

The most significant data limitation arises in comparing the effectiveness of different incentive types. The Olsberg SPI Global Incentives Index 2025 only provides very high-level comparable characterisations of incentive schemes, abstracting the features that determine their effectiveness in practice. The Model~4 and Model~5 estimates should be interpreted with this caveat in mind. Furthermore, subnational incentives are not captured in this analysis.

\subsection{Identification and Generalisability Limitations}
\label{sec:lim_identification}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

There are three main limitations regarding identification and validity of the results. First, the country-pair fixed effects design absorbs all time-invariant bilateral heterogeneity but cannot rule out time-varying confounders. If incentive adoption coincides with other policy or structural changes, the pair fixed effects estimator cannot separate the two. To the extent that incentive adoption is correlated with such time-varying policy changes, the pair fixed effects estimates conflate the two. Second, the sample period is dominated by the pre-streaming theatrical era. However, production location decisions in the streaming era are made by different decision-makers operating under different commercial logic, potentially limiting the applicability of these results.

Third, estimated late-adopter effects may overstate the marginal returns available to countries considering adoption today. The Model~7 finding that early adopters attracted substantially more production than late adopters likely reflects diminishing marginal returns in an increasingly crowded policy environment. However, a country considering adoption today faces an even more saturated policy landscape than the late adopters in this sample did and presumably even smaller marginal returns from introducing an incentive scheme.
