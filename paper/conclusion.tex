
This paper has examined whether national film production incentive schemes attract international film production, using a gravity model framework. I find that the gravity framework fits bilateral film production flows well but the determinants differ substantially from those documented for goods trade. I also find evidence that incentive schemes are effective at attracting international film productions (roughly $9-11\%$ under country-pair fixed effects for film counts), and weaker evidence that incentives lead to production returning home. However, when it comes to attracting film production, the timing of adoption matters more than the duration of operation, with early adopters attracting substantially more production than late adopters and no strong evidence of a cumulative "cluster effect". Taken together, these findings indicate a genuine treatment effect rather than pure selection, but the magnitude is substantially smaller than cross-sectional estimates imply. Furthermore, the incentive effect appears to be a one-time level shift dependent mainly on early entry, rather than a compounding advantage that grows with time and can be harnessed anytime. A country considering adoption today faces a saturated landscape in which the marginal effect of an additional scheme is plausibly smaller than even the late-adopter estimates reported here. 

Two potential extensions exist. First, complementary application of the group-time average treatment effect estimator of \citet{callaway2021} would directly test the cohort-specific effect heterogeneity that Model~7 identifies. Second, a more comprehensive treatment of the different incentive schemes available, both by including subnational incentive schemes in the analysis and by more finely categorising the characteristics of different incentive schemes so that they can be appropriately compared to one another. These extensions would help further address how effective incentives are and identify which design elements are particularly effective, providing a more complete picture for policymakers.
