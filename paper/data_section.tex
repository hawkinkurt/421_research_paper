\subsection{Bilateral Trade Flow Construction}
\label{sec:data_flows}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

The primary dataset used for this analysis is sourced from the Internet Movie Database (IMDb) and contains the 600 top-grossing films per year from 1990--2023, yielding approximately 20,400 films. Each film contains metadata such as their name, countries of origin, primary filming location, budget, and release year. However, coverage of key variables differs across the panel. While countries of origin are available for most films, filming location data is available for approximately 70\% of films. Furthermore, a lower proportion of non-English-language productions have budgets in USD, so the budget data underrepresents non-English-language productions. This limitation is addressed through the use of subsamples for the Fixed Effects specifications that exclude the English-speaking Anglophone countries (United States, Canada, United Kingdom, Australia, and New Zealand).

Filming locations are extracted by parsing IMDb address strings, taking the final comma-separated element as the country name (e.g., ``Winnetka, Illinois, USA'' yields ``United States''). A comprehensive mapping file harmonises over 160 variant country names and sub-national entities to standardized ISO country codes. For each film with a valid filming country and at least one country of origin, I construct bilateral flows from each origin country to the filming country. Only films which contain non-indentical countries of origin and filming locations are retained, thereby excluding purely domestic productions. This leaves approximately 7,579 films. 

Two dependent variables are constructed at the country-pair-year level. The primary dependent variable is the number of films produced per year, while the secondary dependent variable is the total production budgets in constant 2010 USD, which is available for approximately 47\% of films. For films with multiple countries of origin, each origin is paired with this single filming country to generate a separate bilateral flow. When a film has multiple countries of origin, the full budget is divided equally across origin countries.

As a robustness check, I also test using the OECD--WTO Balanced Trade in Services (BaTIS) `Audiovisual and related services data which captures bilateral exports of film, television, radio, and related production services from 2005 to 2023. While the BaTIS data captures non-film related audiovisual services trade, it also captures other filmmaking related activity such as post-production, visual effects, and animation work. While this activity is not being directly captured by the IMDb data (which relies on physical filming locations), it can also be expected to be closely intertwined with physical filming location decisions and therefore respond to similar factors such as film incentives, making it an appropriate robustness check. As with film budgets, these values are deflated to constant 2010 USD.

\subsection{Gravity Variables}
\label{sec:data_gravity}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

Standard bilateral gravity variables are sourced from the CEPII GeoDist database and include bilateral distance (population-weighted, in logs), contiguity, common official language, colonial relationship post-1945, and regional trade agreements. Where the CEPII data coverage ends at 2020, the most recent available year is forward-filled to 2023 under the assumption that these predominantly time-invariant variables change minimally over this extension period.

The Economic Freedom of the World (EFW) index data is sourced from the Fraser Institute, both measured for importer and exporter. The EFW index is published annually only from 2000 onward; prior to 2000, it is published at five-year intervals. To obtain a complete annual panel for the 1990--2023 period, linear interpolation is applied within each country's time series for the missing years, as is standard in the literature using EFW data.

Annual GDP and inflation data is sourced from the World Bank. The GDP data is deflated to constant 2015 US dollars, with the natural logarithm of GDP being used in the gravity specification.

\subsection{Film Incentive Data}
\label{sec:data_incentives}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

Data on national film production incentive schemes for 63 countries is sourced from the Olsberg SPI Global Incentives Index 2025 \citep{olsberg2025}. For each country, the dataset records the year of incentive introduction, the headline rebate or credit rate (as a percentage of qualifying production expenditure), and the incentive type. Incentive types follow the three-category classification used by Olsberg SPI: \textbf{rebates}, which return a percentage of qualifying production expenditure as cash grants; \textbf{tax credits}, which offer a reduction against corporate tax owed; and \textbf{tax shelters}, which are funded by investors seeking tax relief in exchange for financing production. Each country is assigned a single category corresponding to its primary national scheme and a rate as a \% of production costs. Of the 57 countries with an active national incentive scheme in the dataset, 48 operate a rebate, 6 operate a tax credit, and 3 operate a tax shelter. In order to fit within the country-apir framework, subnational incentives are excluded from the analysis and countries with subnational schemes (such as the U.S) but lack a national scheme are categorised as not having an incentive scheme. Where Olsberg reports tiered or variable headline rates (e.g., rates that vary by expenditure band or include uplifts for local hiring), the midpoint across tiers is used.
